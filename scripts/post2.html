How to Get Charity Bibs

Most major marathons have two main paths: general entry (lottery or time-qualifying) and charity entries. Understanding those rules is half the battle.

1. Start With a Cause That Truly Matters to You

Before you think about forms and deadlines, start with your "why." Charities notice when your application is deeply personal versus generic.

2. Learn How Each Race Handles Charity Bibs

Not every major works the same way:

  • For races like New York, you can try the lottery first, and if you don't get in, turn to charity partners.
  • Tokyo is different—the charity selection happens before the general lottery, and it's more like a bidding process.

3. Be Concrete About Your Fundraising Commitment

When you apply, go beyond "I'll raise the minimum" and outline:

  • What amount you're committing to
  • Where that money is likely to come from
  • Any prior fundraising you've successfully done

4. Write a Strong "Why I Want to Run for You" Paragraph

Your story matters—sometimes more than the number. Be specific, honest, and clear about why you want to represent this charity at this race.

5. Reach Out Early to Charity Partners

Early contact helps you get a longer runway to fundraise and puts you on their radar as someone genuinely interested.

Proven Fundraising Strategies That Work

1. Educate People About Your Cause

Don't just drop a donation link. Share meaningful facts or stories about your charity in your posts, emails, and conversations.

2. Make the Ask Simple and Low-Friction

Robin's approach: "If everyone reading this donated $5, that would make a huge difference." Use one clear number, pair it with a one-sentence explanation, and let people know there's no guilt if they can't give.

3. Give People Something in Exchange

Create simple merchandise—shirts, hats, stickers—tied to your race. It's still a donation but feels more like buying something cool from a friend.

4. Host Events That Create Value and Community

Host a small gathering: a silent auction, movie night, or themed party. Events give people an excuse to gather and make giving feel like a shared experience.

5. Don't Underestimate the Power of Your Workplace

Your coworkers may not be getting hit with as many donation requests and might have access to company giving programs or matching.

6. Thank Every Donor Personally

Send a quick DM, text, email, or handwritten note to each donor. This turns donors into long-term supporters.

Key Takeaway

Charity running isn't just about the miles or the money – it's about community, purpose, and the joy of running for something bigger than yourself.
