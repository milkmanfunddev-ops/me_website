Dreaming of running a World Marathon Major but don't have a qualifying time—or struck out in the lottery? Charity entries are often the most reliable way in. This guide covers fundraising minimums, application windows, and key deadlines for all six majors.

Boston Marathon (April)

Boston is both the hardest to qualify for and one of the most expensive to access via charity. The B.A.A.'s own charity team requires at least US$8,500. Realistically, expect US$7,500–$10,000+.

Individual charities open applications on their own schedules but often recruit from fall through early winter for the race the following April.

TCS New York City Marathon (November)

NYC might be the most charity-friendly of all the majors. NYRR requires a minimum of at least US$3,500 per runner. Plan for US$3,500–$5,000 as a realistic band.

The lottery usually runs in February with results in early March. Charity partners recruit more aggressively after lottery results.

Bank of America Chicago Marathon (October)

Chicago offers one of the lowest fundraising minimums among the majors—at least US$2,200. Expect US$2,200–$2,500 as your baseline.

When general entries close, charity entries remain one of the last ways in.

BMW Berlin Marathon (September)

Berlin is popular for its fast course. Fundraising minimums are generally in the US$3,000–$4,000 range. Many international charity partners recruit 9–12 months before race day.

TCS London Marathon (April)

London is legendary for charity running, regularly setting global records for one-day fundraising. A safe planning band is £2,000–£3,000+ for a charity-place runner.

The public ballot typically opens right after the current year's race and stays open for about a week.

Tokyo Marathon (March)

Tokyo is structurally different: the charity process happens before the general lottery and works more like a silent auction. The minimum donation is JPY 100,000 (about US$650–$700), but typical successful bids exceed the minimum significantly.

The charity entry window typically runs for a few weeks in late June to mid-July.

How to Use This Guide

  1. Pick your race, then work backwards from the race date.
  2. Estimate your fundraising capacity—first-timers might aim for Chicago or Berlin.
  3. Research 3–5 charities per race—look up minimums, support offered, and application competitiveness.
  4. Watch for milestones and deadlines—many charities require interim goals.
  5. Keep your story at the center—your personal connection matters as much as the number.
